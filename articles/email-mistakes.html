Email Mistakes That Instantly Make You Look Unprofessional

Every email you send is a micro-impression. Sent to the wrong people, in the wrong tone, with the wrong subject line — it forms a judgment about you that lingers long after the email is closed.

Mihir Madhuvan | Think Like HR | 9 min read | Workplace Etiquette

You probably send 20–50 emails on a busy workday. That's 20–50 small moments where people are forming an impression of your professionalism, attention to detail, and communication skills.

Most of those impressions go unnoticed. A handful are remembered — for the wrong reasons.

In corporate India, where relationships, hierarchy, and communication norms matter enormously, email mistakes carry real consequences. They affect how managers see you. How clients treat you. Whether HR flags you as someone with "communication issues."

Here's what to stop doing immediately.

Key Takeaways

  • Email tone and structure directly shape how professional you appear to seniors and clients
  • Common mistakes — Reply All, vague subject lines, passive-aggressive closing — are career reputation risks
  • In India's corporate culture, how you communicate with hierarchy matters as much as content
  • Emails are permanent records — what you write can resurface in disputes, reviews, or investigations
  • Most email mistakes are completely fixable with a checklist and 30 extra seconds before sending

1. Vague Subject Lines That Tell Nobody Anything

Subject: "Check this"
Subject: "FWD: FWD: RE: RE: Update"
Subject: "Query"

These are not subject lines. They're placeholders. And they signal that the sender doesn't respect the reader's time.

A good subject line tells the reader what the email is about and what action (if any) is needed. It answers the question: why should I open this now?

❌ Weak Subject: Query about leave
✓ Clear Subject: Leave request — 15–17 July [Action Required]

2. Hitting Reply All When It Should Be a One-on-One

Someone sends a message to a 12-person thread. You reply to everyone with: "Thanks! On it." Or worse: "I think this is actually Rajan's responsibility, not mine."

Reply All is one of the most common ways people accidentally create office politics. Before hitting that button, ask: does everyone on this thread need to see this reply? Almost always, the answer is no.

If you need to have a sensitive conversation, have it directly. Not through 12 CC'd witnesses.

3. Writing Passive-Aggressive Sign-Offs

Some email endings are professionally neutral. Others carry a very specific tone:

  • "As mentioned earlier..." — implies the reader wasn't paying attention
  • "Hope this helps." — fine on its own, but read differently after a tense exchange
  • "Please revert at the earliest." — widely used in Indian offices but borders on demanding
  • "Please note." — can come across as a mild reproach depending on context

These phrases aren't wrong. But read them from the recipient's perspective. If there's any tension in the thread, they land harder than you intend.

4. Sending Emotional Emails in Real Time

Something frustrating just happened. A deadline was missed. A client was rude. A colleague passed the blame. And you sat down and wrote an email about exactly how you felt.

This is one of the most damaging email habits that exists in professional environments. Emotional emails feel satisfying to write and almost always create lasting problems.

"Write the email. Save it as a draft. Sleep on it. In the morning, you'll either send a much better version — or realise you shouldn't have sent anything at all."

If you receive an email that provokes a strong reaction, wait before responding. Minimum one hour. Complex situations deserve a day. A conversation is almost always better than an email for anything sensitive.

5. Using CC and BCC as Power Moves

CC-ing your manager on every email to a colleague — especially in disputes — sends a clear message: I don't trust you and I want oversight involved. It poisons working relationships fast.

BCC-ing someone on a sensitive thread without the other party knowing is even more fraught. If it's ever discovered (and it often is), you've created a trust problem that's very hard to recover from.

Use CC for genuine information-sharing. Use BCC sparingly, and only when there's a clear, non-political reason for it.

6. Emails That Are Wall-to-Wall Text

Long, dense paragraphs in email are hard to read on a computer screen. They're nearly impossible on a phone. When a senior leader or client opens an email and sees a 400-word paragraph with no structure, they often do one of two things:

  • Skim it and miss what you needed them to see
  • Set it aside to "read properly later" — which sometimes means never

Use short paragraphs. Use bullet points for multiple items. Put the most important point first, not last. Make the required action explicit and separate.

7. Not Addressing the Right Person at the Right Seniority Level

Indian corporate culture has strong hierarchy norms. How you address people in email signals whether you understand this.

Emailing a VP with "Hey Rohit" when you've never met them is jarring. Addressing a peer you've known for years as "Dear Mr. Sharma" is weirdly formal and creates distance.

General guidance:

  • First communication with senior leaders: "Dear [Name]" or "Hi [Name]," depending on company culture
  • Ongoing communication with peers: "Hi [First Name]," is standard
  • External clients or vendors you don't know well: err on the formal side until they signal otherwise
  • Never open with just a name and comma: "Anita," on its own as an opener reads as cold or accusatory

8. Forgetting to Add the Attachment Before Sending

This one seems minor. But "please find attached" followed by an email with no attachment — especially to a client or senior leader — is remembered. It happens to everyone occasionally, but repeat offenders develop a reputation for carelessness that sticks.

Simple fix: attach the document before you write the email. Not after. Do the attachment first.

9. Writing Emails You Wouldn't Want Screenshotted

Every email you send on a company account is potentially visible to HR, legal, or leadership under the right circumstances. This isn't paranoia. It's how corporate email systems work.

Don't put in an email what you wouldn't say in a meeting. Don't document internal frustrations, gossip, or criticism of colleagues or clients in writing. Don't make commitments in email without thinking about whether you can keep them.

When in doubt: would I be comfortable if this email was read by my skip-level at the worst possible moment? If not, rewrite it.

10. Excessive Formality That Slows Everything Down

Over-formality is also a mistake. Writing three-paragraph preambles before asking a simple question. Restating the entire history of a project every time. Adding unnecessary pleasantries to quick operational emails.

In fast-moving teams, clarity beats politeness when they're in conflict. A short, clear, direct email is not rude. It's respectful of everyone's time.

Match your email style to the context: formal for sensitive topics, external contacts, or escalations; direct for day-to-day team communication.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it unprofessional to email in lowercase or use casual language with close colleagues?
Depends on your team culture and who's on the thread. A casual internal Slack or Teams message is different from a formal email. But if you're CC-ing leadership, clients, or HR — even informally — keep the register professional. When you're unsure who might eventually see an email, write as if the most formal person on your contact list is reading it.
How should I handle an email where someone has made a mistake and I need to address it?
Fact-based, not blame-based. "I noticed the report shared on Monday had figures from the previous quarter — I've updated it here for clarity." Not: "You sent the wrong numbers again." The first version informs. The second creates defensiveness and a bad interaction that festers in an inbox.
Is "please revert" correct English? My Indian colleagues all use it.
It's commonly used in Indian corporate English but technically incorrect. "Revert" means to return to a previous state — it doesn't mean "reply." The correct phrasing is "please respond" or "please reply." In a domestic-only context it's usually understood, but in any international communication, avoid it.
When should I escalate something by email vs call vs in-person?
Email for documentation, confirmation, and non-urgent issues. Call or message for quick clarity and coordination. In-person (or video) for anything sensitive, ambiguous, or likely to cause tension. The more emotional or complex a situation, the less suitable email becomes as the medium.
My company expects 24/7 email responsiveness. Is that normal?
Increasingly common, rarely written into contracts, and worth understanding. Know your company's implicit norms. If senior leadership sends emails at 11pm and expects responses, that's the culture signal. If they don't, you're not obligated to perform availability. The key is to be clear about your availability — not to silently resent the expectation while meeting it.

Your Email is Your Professional Brand

In an office, you can explain, clarify, and course-correct in real time. In email, the words sit there — unfiltered, permanent, and interpreted without your tone of voice or body language.

Most email mistakes aren't catastrophic. But they accumulate. A colleague who consistently sends vague, passive-aggressive, or careless emails gets quietly labelled — in ways they often never find out about directly.

Before you hit send: read it once more. From their perspective. Not yours.

That one habit changes everything.
