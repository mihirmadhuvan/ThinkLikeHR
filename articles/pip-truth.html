The Truth About PIPs Nobody Explains

A Performance Improvement Plan sounds like a second chance. Most of the time, the decision has already been made before you receive it.

By Mihir Madhuvan 10 min read HR Secrets

You walk into a meeting with your manager and HR. Everyone looks uncomfortable. Then they slide a document across the table — a Performance Improvement Plan.

Your heart sinks. You're told this is a "structured opportunity to get back on track." You're told the company believes in you. You're told this is not a termination notice.

What they don't tell you is this: in most Indian companies, a PIP is the last step before an exit. The paperwork has to exist before the company can legally let someone go.

This article is everything HR knows about PIPs — and everything you deserve to know.

Key Takeaways
  • A PIP is almost always a documentation process, not a development process
  • HR initiates PIPs to create a legal paper trail before termination
  • Most PIPs are designed to be extremely difficult to pass
  • You have rights during a PIP — most employees don't know them
  • There are smart ways to respond that protect your career

What a PIP Actually Is

On paper, a Performance Improvement Plan is a formal document that outlines specific performance issues, measurable targets, and a timeline to meet those targets — usually 30, 60, or 90 days.

It reads like a support system. Bi-weekly check-ins. Clear objectives. Manager feedback loops.

In practice, a PIP is created by HR and the manager together — often before you even know there is a performance issue on record.

The targets inside a PIP are usually set just above what you can realistically achieve in the time given. This isn't an accident.

Why Companies Issue PIPs

Indian employment law doesn't allow arbitrary termination of permanent employees. If a company wants to let someone go without paying full severance or risking a labour complaint, it needs documented evidence of sustained poor performance.

The PIP creates that documentation.

Think of it from HR's perspective:

  • If the employee fails the PIP — the company has grounds to terminate
  • If the employee resigns during the PIP — the company saves severance costs
  • If somehow the employee passes — they keep a functioning worker

All three outcomes work in the company's favour.

The Inside View

HR professionals often refer to PIPs as "managed exits." The goal isn't improvement — it's documentation that makes a clean exit possible.

The Signs a PIP Is Real vs. a Paper Exit

Not every PIP is a death sentence. Some managers genuinely want to help an underperforming team member succeed. But the signs of a genuine PIP versus a documentation exercise are usually obvious — once you know what to look for.

Signs it might be genuine:

  • Your manager seems uncomfortable, not detached
  • The targets are specific, measurable, and achievable within the timeline
  • Training, resources, or mentorship is actually provided
  • Your manager still includes you in team decisions
  • HR follows up but isn't watching your every move

Signs it's a managed exit:

  • The targets are vague ("improve client satisfaction") or impossibly high
  • Your access to systems or projects quietly reduces
  • Your manager stops including you in important meetings
  • HR is unusually present in day-to-day matters
  • The document was clearly prepared well in advance
  • You're given 30 days when the goal would normally take 6 months
Real Workplace Scenario

Priya, a 4-year sales manager at a mid-size Pune firm, received a PIP in October. Her target: increase regional revenue by 25% in 60 days — during the slow Diwali season.

She worked 12-hour days. She hit 19%. She was terminated in December.

The company had already hired her replacement in November — two weeks into her PIP period.

What HR Does Before You Receive the PIP

Most employees think the PIP comes as a surprise. It doesn't — not from HR's side.

Before you receive that document, here is what has already happened:

  • Your manager has had multiple conversations with HR about you
  • HR has reviewed your employment contract and notice period
  • Legal or compliance has reviewed the PIP draft
  • The targets in your PIP have been calculated — sometimes reverse-engineered to be achievable enough to look fair but difficult enough to fail
  • A timeline for your potential exit has been internally discussed

You are the last person to know. That asymmetry of information is something you must understand.

Your Rights During a PIP

Most employees sign the PIP document without reading it carefully. Some are told that signing is mandatory. It isn't — not always.

Here is what you need to know:

  • You can ask for clarification on every target before signing
  • You can request resources — training, tools, managerial support — in writing
  • You do not have to sign immediately — ask for 24–48 hours to review
  • You can add a note to the signed document disagreeing with specific points
  • You can document everything — every check-in, every email, every promised support that doesn't materialise

Documentation is your only protection. Use it.

Should You Try to Pass It or Plan an Exit?

This is the question nobody answers directly. Here is the honest answer: it depends entirely on which type of PIP you're in.

If the PIP appears genuine — targets are fair, manager is supportive — give it everything you have. Some people do survive PIPs and rebuild strong careers in the same company.

If the PIP is clearly designed to fail — start your job search immediately. Use the PIP period to interview quietly, negotiate a better exit, and protect your next opportunity.

Never resign impulsively during a PIP. That is exactly what the company wants. If you resign, you lose all entitlement to severance and may lose some benefits. If they terminate you, you have stronger legal standing.

The Smart Move

Update your LinkedIn privately. Contact your recruiter network quietly. Give your best effort on paper — while planning your next chapter in parallel. You owe it to yourself.

How a PIP Affects Your Full and Final Settlement

If you are terminated after failing a PIP, your Full and Final (FnF) settlement usually includes:

  • Salary for days worked in the last month
  • Pending leave encashment
  • Gratuity (if applicable — 5 years of service)
  • PF settlement
  • Notice pay (or waiver of notice, depending on your contract)

Make sure you receive the FnF amount before signing any separation agreement or releasing the company from future claims.

The Emotional Cost Nobody Talks About

A PIP doesn't just affect your job. It affects how you see yourself at work.

You start second-guessing decisions. You over-explain every email. You feel watched. You feel like a liability instead of a contributor.

That psychological pressure is partly by design. When people are under stress, they make mistakes — and those mistakes feed back into the performance documentation.

The most important thing you can do during a PIP is maintain perspective. One company's opinion of your performance is not the truth about your worth or your future.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I refuse to sign a PIP?
You can refuse, but it can be used against you. A smarter approach: sign with a written note stating you "acknowledge receipt but dispute specific targets" — this protects you legally while avoiding an outright refusal.
Does a PIP show up on background checks?
No. PIPs are internal documents and do not appear on background verification checks. However, employment termination for performance reasons may be disclosed by a previous employer if contacted directly — which is why managing your references matters.
What's the difference between a warning letter and a PIP?
A warning letter is a one-time formal notice. A PIP is a structured process over weeks with targets and check-ins. A PIP carries significantly more legal weight as documentation for potential termination.
Can I negotiate during a PIP?
Yes — and many employees don't realise this. You can negotiate the targets, the timeline, and even negotiate a mutual separation with agreed severance instead of going through the PIP process. HR often prefers clean exits over prolonged performance battles.
If I pass the PIP, am I safe?
Not necessarily. Some employees who pass a PIP find they're put on another one within 6 months, or quietly managed out through other means. Passing a PIP when the company wanted an exit can extend the timeline but rarely changes the underlying decision.

Final Word

A PIP is not a performance tool. In most cases, it's an exit tool with a performance costume on.

That doesn't mean you should panic. It means you should be clear-eyed. Understand what you're in. Know your rights. Document everything. And move with intention — not reaction.

The company has been planning this conversation for weeks. You deserve to be just as prepared.
