HR Secrets

What HR departments really do, how they think, and what they know about you that you don't.

HR is often misunderstood. Employees think HR is there for them. In reality, HR's primary job is to protect the organisation — not the individual employee. Understanding how HR actually works helps you navigate your career with your eyes open, communicate smarter, and avoid costly mistakes.

🏢 What Does HR Actually Do?

Human Resources is responsible for managing the employee lifecycle — from hiring to exit. But beyond the visible functions, HR operates with tools, data and processes most employees are unaware of.

Talent Acquisition

Sourcing, screening and hiring candidates. HR uses ATS (Applicant Tracking Systems) that filter resumes before a human ever reads them.

Performance Management

HR tracks performance data — attendance, KPIs, manager ratings, peer feedback — and uses it in decisions far beyond your annual review.

Compensation & Benefits

HR designs salary structures, CTC breakdowns and benefit policies — often in ways that maximise company savings while appearing generous.

Compliance & Risk

HR ensures the company stays legally compliant. When there is a conflict between employee interest and legal/reputational risk, compliance wins.

👁 What HR Can See About You

Modern HR departments have access to more employee data than most people realise. In large organisations, this includes:

  • Login and logout times from office systems or VPN
  • Email metadata (who you communicate with, how often) in company accounts
  • Badge swipe data showing when you enter and exit the building
  • Your performance ratings from every review cycle — even ones never shared with you
  • Manager notes and escalations stored in HRMS tools
  • Your activity on internal platforms like Slack, Teams or Jira
  • Training completion rates and learning platform activity

📋 The Truth About Performance Improvement Plans (PIPs)

A PIP — Performance Improvement Plan — is framed as an opportunity to improve. In most Indian companies, it functions very differently.

What a PIP Usually Means

In most cases, a PIP is the formal beginning of a managed exit. The documentation created during a PIP protects the company legally if they terminate the employee later. Very few employees successfully "pass" a PIP and continue with the same career trajectory. If you're placed on a PIP, begin updating your resume immediately while complying professionally with all PIP requirements.

  • Ask for the PIP document in writing — verbal PIPs have no legal standing
  • Request clarity on measurable targets and timelines before signing
  • Keep copies of all communication related to your PIP
  • Never resign immediately upon receiving a PIP — you may lose severance entitlements
  • If targets seem impossible to meet, document that observation in writing

🗣 How to Talk to HR Smartly

HR conversations require strategy. Here's what experienced professionals know:

  • HR is not your friend or confidant — anything you share can be used in decisions about your employment
  • Always follow up verbal HR conversations with a written email summary ("As discussed today…")
  • Never share personal frustrations, salary comparisons with colleagues, or intentions to leave casually with HR
  • When asking for salary corrections or benefits, cite policy documents and market data — not personal need
  • If you raise a complaint, do it in writing to create a formal record
  • Understand that HR's goal in conflict situations is to minimise company liability, not deliver justice

🚪 Exit Interviews: What HR Doesn't Tell You

Exit interviews are conducted by HR to gather data. They are rarely used to fix problems for existing employees. What you say in an exit interview can affect your relieving letter, reference letter and final settlement.

What You Should Say

Keep it professional and neutral. Mention growth opportunities at the new role. Avoid criticising specific managers or colleagues by name.

What You Should Not Say

Avoid venting about salary, unfair treatment, or team politics — even if true. These comments rarely drive change and can delay your exit formalities.

📁 Your Permanent Record: What HR Keeps

Many employees don't realise that their HR file contains a detailed history that can follow them — especially in industries where background verification is thorough.

  • All offer letters, increment letters and promotion documents
  • Disciplinary records, warning letters and PIPs
  • Manager performance reviews and 360-degree feedback
  • Attendance and leave records going back years
  • Resignation and exit documentation
  • Background verification results from your joining
