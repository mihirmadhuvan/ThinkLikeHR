Salary & Benefits

Decode your CTC, understand your deductions and make sure you're not losing money you're entitled to.

Most employees in India negotiate their CTC without fully understanding what it means. They accept offers, sign letters and spend years not knowing they are losing PF contributions, gratuity and tax benefits they're fully entitled to. This guide changes that.

📊 CTC vs In-Hand Salary: The Difference Nobody Explains

CTC — Cost to Company — is the total amount your employer spends on you annually. Your in-hand salary is what actually hits your bank account. The gap between the two can be 20–40% and it's important to understand where that money goes.

⚠ The Basic Pay Trap

The most expensive salary mistake employees make is accepting a low Basic Pay. Basic Pay is the foundation of your PF contribution and your gratuity. Companies sometimes inflate allowances and keep Basic Pay low — which looks good on paper but quietly costs you lakhs over your career.

The New 50% Rule Under Labour Codes

Under India's new Labour Codes (once fully implemented), Basic Pay must be at least 50% of your CTC. This is a major shift. If your current Basic is only 20–30% of CTC, your PF contribution and future gratuity are significantly lower than they should be. Always negotiate Basic Pay, not just total CTC.

🏦 PF: How It Works & Common Mistakes

Your PF (Provident Fund) is a long-term retirement corpus that grows tax-free. But most employees have never checked their PF balance or verified whether their employer is depositing correctly.

  • You contribute 12% of Basic Pay; employer contributes another 12%
  • The employer's 12% is split: 8.33% to Pension (EPS) and 3.67% to PF
  • Check your balance via EPFO portal (epfindia.gov.in) or the Umang app
  • If your employer deducts PF but doesn't deposit it — that is a punishable offence
  • Withdrawing PF before 5 years of continuous service makes it taxable
  • You can transfer PF when switching jobs using your UAN number

🎁 Gratuity: Your Silent Wealth Builder

Gratuity is a lump-sum benefit paid by employers when you complete 5 or more years of service. It is your right under the Payment of Gratuity Act — not a bonus or gift.

Formula

(Last Basic + DA) × 15 × Years of Service ÷ 26

Example: ₹30,000 basic, 6 years = ₹30,000 × 15 × 6 ÷ 26 = ₹1,03,846

Eligibility

Minimum 5 years of continuous service with the same employer. Even 4 years and 240 working days in the 5th year may qualify in some interpretations.

Tax Treatment

Gratuity up to ₹20 lakhs is fully tax-exempt for private sector employees. Beyond that, it is taxable as per your income slab.

If Employer Refuses

File a complaint with the Controlling Authority (Labour Commissioner) in your area. Withholding gratuity is a legal violation.

🏠 HRA: Are You Claiming It Correctly?

House Rent Allowance (HRA) is one of the biggest legal tax-saving opportunities for salaried employees. Yet many employees either don't claim it or claim less than they're entitled to.

  • HRA exemption is the lowest of: actual HRA received, 50% of basic (metro) or 40% (non-metro), or actual rent paid minus 10% of basic
  • You must submit rent receipts (and landlord PAN if annual rent exceeds ₹1 lakh) to your employer
  • You can claim HRA even if you pay rent to a parent — but keep documentation proper
  • If your employer doesn't offer HRA, you can still claim under Section 80GG while filing ITR

📋 Benefits Most Employees Never Use

LTA (Leave Travel Allowance)

Tax-exempt for travel within India. Can be claimed twice in a block of 4 years. Requires actual travel and tickets as proof.

Food Coupons / Meal Card

Up to ₹50 per meal (approx ₹26,400/year) is tax-exempt. If your employer offers this, opt in — it's essentially tax-free cash.

NPS (National Pension Scheme)

Employer's contribution to NPS (up to 10% of salary) is exempt from tax under Section 80CCD(2) — over and above the ₹1.5L 80C limit.

Medical Reimbursement

If your company has a medical reimbursement policy, submit bills and claim them. Unclaimed medical benefits are a common silent loss.
